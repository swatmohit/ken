RBI’s extended moratorium a triple whammy for NBFCs

India needs non-banking financial companies the most now, but the RBI’s six-month moratorium has put enormous operational pressure on them. NBFCs are staring at a high mortality risk brought on by diluted credit culture and uncertain repayment behaviour

Arundhati Ramanathan, 29 May 2020

NBFCs, which disburse 20% of credit in India, have a big role in propping up the economy after Covid-19

At least 20-25% of India’s NBFCs could face significant stress because of the RBI’s moratorium

Anywhere between 20-90% of all borrowers availed the moratorium, throwing non-bank lenders’ operations into disarray

At risk is the carefully groomed credit culture and borrower behaviour

“Normandy.”

That’s how fintech lender RedCarpet’s founder Sandeep Srinivasa described the situation India’s non-bank lenders are in today. 

Srinivasa was referring to the pivotal moment in World War II, when the allied forces stunned Germany by invading Normandy, France. He sees non-banking financial companies (NBFCs) playing the role of the allied troops in the battle against the economic slowdown. They need to be armed to fire up the economy. But instead, they are at risk of being cannon fodder, with banks being prioritised. 

“NBFCs have to make their way into a new post-Covid world without air support from banks and regulators,” said Srinivasa. “If we make it to the other side, we will drink French wine. But a lot of us won’t make it.”

NBFCs lend close to 20% of all credit in India and have traditionally traditionally The Economic Times Crisil: NBFC credit growth will slow to a decade low of 6% Read more contributed to 35% of all new lending. The small and mid-sized NBFCs have been particularly active in increasing credit penetration, especially to borrowers banks won’t touch. Banks mostly lend to their own account holders, the salaried class, and those who have a stellar credit score. And they deal with larger loan sizes. 

NBFCs already limped into the pandemic period, wounded by the IL&FS IL&FS The Economic Times IL&FS: The crisis that has India in panic mode Read more  crisis. When the infrastructure lender defaulted on payments to lenders in 2018, it resulted in reduced liquidity for NBFCs. In the year ended March 2020, NBFCs saw their lowest growth in a decade.

That outlook only worsened with the onset of the Covid-19 panic, even with the intervention of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), the country’s central bank. 

Soon after the pandemic paralysed the economy, the RBI allowed all lending institutions to offer a three-month moratorium moratorium Moratorium A temporary measure to let borrowers not pay their EMIs for a specific period of time. But interest liabilities will continue to accrue in that period.  on loan repayments, starting 1 March. This was a double-edged sword. On one hand, it spared NBFCs the burden of defaults and non-performing assets (NPAs). On the other hand, banks didn’t extend this courtesy to NBFCs.

Last week, things got direr. The repayment moratorium was extended by three months—till 31 August. It was a triple whammy. Beyond the enormous operational pressure on NBFCs, this could change the credit culture and repayment behaviour of borrowers altogether. Anywhere between 20-90% of all borrowers in banks and NBFCs availed the RBI’s moratorium. 

The first moratorium was necessary and provided much-needed relief to borrowers and lenders, said Rangarajan K, CEO of Five Star Finance, an NBFC, which has disbursed close to Rs 4,000 crore. However, the second moratorium, if abused, could actually become a “mortalitorium”—a portmanteau of mortality and moratorium, coined by Rangarajan.

At least 20-25% of the country’s NBFCs could face significant stress, possibly even an existential crisis, because of this, said the CFO of a Chennai-based NBFC. With India’s GDP headed headed Mint I-bank says projected fall in FY21 GDP will be deeper than India’s biggest recessions Read more  for a 5% fall in the year ending March 2021, according to investment bank Goldman Sachs, the plight of non-bank lenders is particularly worrying. 

The “mortalitorium”

The moratorium applies to all kinds of loans: from credit cards to personal loans, to home loans, and corporate loans. But it isn’t legally binding. Lenders can choose to not roll out the moratorium at all or offer it selectively. However, it is in the lender’s own interest to allow deferred payments, avoiding the burden of having defaults on their books.

What NBFCs soon realised was that their loan management systems were inadequate to deal with the complexity of deferred payments. “I’m writing and rewriting code for calculation of Days Past Due of all our borrowers, and interest borrowers have to pay,” said RedCarpet’s Srinivasa. Around 25% of RedCarpet’s assets are under moratorium. 

Long tail of credit

For small and medium-sized NBFCs, the loan size ranges between Rs 25,000 and Rs 25 lakh, usually for a period of 3-5 years. And the NPAs on these loans range from 1-10%.

NBFCs went about implementing the moratorium differently. While some let their borrowers opt-out of the moratorium, rolling it out to everyone else, others chose an opt-in approach. For PaySense (acquired by payment gateway PayU), a fintech that disburses loans from four different lenders, this made things more difficult.

“It took over a month to just come up with this system,” said Prashanth Ranganathan, co-founder of PaySense, “The nuances of what each lender wants to do is very different, making it very complex.”

An opt-out strategy is the dicier one. The lender risks its entire borrower base not paying up for six months. Five Star needed to choose this because of the class of borrowers it deals deals The Ken Five Star Finance shows unsecured lending is overrated Read more with. It disburses loans of Rs 3-5 lakh to segments like the self-employed and vegetable sellers, who may not be savvy enough to opt in digitally.

For such NBFCs, communication with borrowers is key. A breakdown could lead to customers not understanding their repayment schedules. The EMIs here just end up getting tagged onto the current tenure, adding more months to the repayment schedule. So when a loan tenure ends after, say, five years, lenders are worried that the collection of additional payments from borrowers’ will grow tougher.

However, with India’s lockdown crossing the two-month mark, there has been an exodus from cities. Many borrowers have gone incommunicado. Curbs on movement have also prevented collection agents from visiting borrowers. 

“We have been able to contact about 60% of our customer base under moratorium,” said Srinivasa of RedCarpet.

Borrowers are just one part of the problem for NBFCs. Liquidity is a more pressing concern.

The unravelling of lenders

The RBI has taken many measures to inject life into these lenders. Among them, a micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSME) loan package of Rs 3,00,000 crore ($39.5 billion) backed by credit guarantees, two sets of TLTROs TLTROs Targeted long-term repo operations RBI sanctioned liquidity facilities to banks for further disbursal. This comes with conditions on how banks can disburse it , and a Rs 30,000 crore ($3.9 billion) liquidity infusion. But NBFCs say that the liquidity hasn’t reached them. 

Either the RBI has routed these measures through banks—and banks, which lend to NBFCs, aren’t passing this on—or the delivery mechanism is flawed.

For instance, the Rs 3,00,000 crore MSME loan package is backed by a credit guarantee. But such schemes have not really worked in the past. It’s not an automatic process where lenders are reimbursed if a loan goes bad, said the CFO of the Chennai-based lender quoted earlier. Similarly, the Rs 30,000 crore liquidity infusion is to allow non-bank lenders to borrow only for three months. NBFCs have written to the finance ministry to extend extend Financial Express NBFC body writes to FM on Rs 75,000 crore schemes, seeks extension of tenure by 3 years Read more this to three years.

“We are facing regulatory discrimination,” said the founder of a Chennai-based NBFC rated as systemically important by RBI. “Banks are [an] essential service. NBFCs are not. It is as basic as that.” 

 The trouble brewing within NBFCs is also easy to overlook because the top 100 companies account for ~80% of the credit. They also have enough liquidity, according to credit rating agency Crisil. It’s the small and medium-sized, unrated NBFCs—which prop up mom-and-pop stores, small auto-parts vendors, or self-employed people—that are under mortal danger.

Crisil’s analysis of the non-bank lenders it rates shows liquidity pressure will increase for nearly a quarter of them if collections do not pick up by June. These NBFCs have Rs 1,75,000 crore ($23 billion) of debt obligations to pay back by then. Already, small and mid-size lenders are staring at the bottom of their liquidity reserves.

Imagine this: If an NBFC expected monthly dues of Rs 300 crore—and this was stalled because of the moratorium—it would need six months worth of liquidity (Rs 1,800 crore, $237 million) in its reserves to tide over this period. 

“Most small and mid-size NBFCs operate with 60-90 days of liquidity reserves,” said Bhavesh Gupta, CEO of Clix Capital. NBFCs don’t stock up on that much liquidity as they would rather lend it than keep it on standby. These reserves are used to meet expenses and pay back lenders.

Without these reserves, NBFCs risk not repaying their lenders—the banks. Private and public banks together make up for over 50% of a small NBFCs’ borrowings. “If NBFCs miss one month of payment, it is rated a D. Once you are D, you won’t get money from banks for nearly a year,” said the founder of a company that helps NBFCs borrow from other financial institutions. 

So, as a result of the moratorium, even the hard-won credit from banks will not go towards lending. “Any liquidity NBFCs raise, will first go towards ensuring enough reserves and conserving cash. Once they have that, then lenders can resume fresh lending,” said Gupta.

That’s not all. 

NBFCs are staring at a major net-worth erosion, which could lead to further rating downgrades. This will make it difficult for them to raise any funding. The RBI says lenders need to set aside 10% of disbursals under moratorium as provisioning for bad loans. This is up from the standard 0.4% provisioning for standard assets that have defaulted by less than 90 days. Essentially, the RBI is asking lenders to treat loans under moratorium as bad assets, at least temporarily.  

“The RBI has said this is a temporary provision,” said Five Star Finance’s Rangarajan. “You can write it back at the end of the year if these assets don’t go bad. It is well-intentioned as you are providing for stress. But the issue is you can breach capital adequacy capital adequacy capital adequacy Promoters/investors of an NBFC fund 15% of an NBFC's assets as a minimum investment to show they have skin in the game and to protect an NBFC assets. norms. You could also end up breaching covenants with existing lenders who have lent based on minimum capital adequacy.”  

NBFCs who chose not to offer the moratorium aren’t better off. They would likely see a spike in NPAs, which would anyway hurt their credit ratings. The moratorium has allowed lenders to kick the proverbial can down the road.

Even NBFCs who have the muscle to lend will see some fundamental shifts. Loan tenures will shorten. Loan sizes will shrink. Fewer people will get loans; and if you are new to credit, your chances are slim to none, said at least six non-bank lenders The Ken spoke with. The very fundamentals of the space are being redrawn.

Foundation: shaken

“The moratorium has caused massive confusion among our borrowers as they believe it is a waiver,” said RedCarpet’s Srinivasa. The NBFC is trying to keep its borrowers updated by issuing reminders on its app about their repayment schedules.  

Lenders also worry that the moratorium comes across as a payment holiday, which is harmful to the credit culture. “A lot of collections in the bottom of the pyramid are based on pull rather than push,” said the CFO of the Chennai-based NBFC quoted earlier. “In those segments, credit culture will break if there is any doubt whether they have to pay.” 

Collections are one half of the problem. To kickstart new lending, NBFCs will have to start almost from scratch. Previous assumptions that formed the cornerstone of their lending may no longer hold good. Who is a good borrower? Someone who has a job in auto manufacturing, a sector that is expected to see no growth? Or someone who lost a job in retail, but has prospects of finding a better job as the sector is not as stressed? For some NBFCs, if a person has taken a moratorium, it is already a black mark.

Even credit bureaus have been rendered irrelevant in these six months. The RBI directed lenders not to report anyone who has availed a moratorium in order to protect their credit scores. As a result, NBFCs also have little idea if borrowers have opted for a moratorium with more than one lender. Typically, borrowers have more than one loan—and as many as three—said Gupta of Clix Capital. 

With these six months a virtual blindspot into borrower behaviour, non-bank lenders are retreating to safer ground. They are likely to cherry-pick from their current set of borrowers based on the data they have, rather than seeking out new users.  

A few others, like PaySense, see this as the year of refinancing. “NBFCs are not making a lot of fresh disbursals. But banks are flush with cash and keen to drive up disbursals,” said Ranganathan. “So we are developing a product that will help borrowers move their loans to banks, which offer a lower interest rate.”

Easing the flight of loans to banks is the interest rate difference that is bound to widen between banks and non-banks. Banks are not dropping the rates at which they lend to NBFCs, even though the RBI slashed repo rates repo rates Repo rate The rate at which RBI lends to banks. A lowered repo rate helps infuse more money into the economy to 4% from 4.4%. “Banks are asking us to transmit 24% interest rates to customers,” said the founder of a Gurugram-based NBFC. “We are asking them to drop the rates to, say, 14%, but banks are not budging.”  

That leaves NBFCs on shakier ground, with good borrowers likely migrating to banks. The moratorium, which was supposed to catalyse economic activity, is maiming NBFCs instead. Their last hope is for an RBI intervention that gives them the required air cover by directing liquidity directly to NBFCs, instead of routing it through banks. Otherwise, much like the thousands of allied forces who died on D-Day, setting the stage for that historic effort, many small and mid-size NBFCs may also perish.
